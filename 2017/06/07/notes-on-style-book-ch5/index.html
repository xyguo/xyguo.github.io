English writing - Cohesion and Coherence

This is the reading notes for book Style - Lessons in Clarity and Grace

Cohesion: The Sense of Flow

Cohesion can be thought as pairs of sentences fitting together in the way two pieces of a jigsaw puzzle do.

Sentences are cohesive when the last few words of one set up information that appears in the first few words of the next. That’s what gives us our experience of flow. and in fact, that’s the biggest reason the passive is in the language: to let us arrange sentences so that they flow from one to the next easily.

Diagnosis and Revision: Old Before New

  1. Begin sentences with information familiar to your readers.
    Readers get that familiar information from two sources. First, they remember words from the sentence they just read. Second, readers bring to a sentence a general knowledge of its subject.
  2. End sentences with information that readers cannot anticipate.
    Readers always prefer to read what’s new and complex after they read what’s familiar and simple.

  • [EXAMPLE]

    • [BAD]: Two aims – the recovery of the American economy and the modernization of America into a military power – were in the president’s mind when he assumed his office. The drop in unemployment figures and inflation, and the increase in the GNP testifies to his success in the first. But our increased involvement in international confilict without any clear set of political goals indicates less success with the second. Nevertheless, increases in the millitary budget and a good deal of saber rattling pleased the American voter.
    • [GOOD]: When the president assumed office, he had two aims – the recovery of the American economy and the modernization of America into a military power. He succeeded in the first, as testified by the drop in unemployment figures and inflation, and the increase in the GNP. But he had less success with the second, as indicated by our increased involvement in international confilict without any clear set of political goals. Nevertheless, the American voter was pleased with increased millitary budget and a good deal of saber rattling.

  • Favor the old-before-new rule when balancing between it and the two clarity rules for individual sentence (i.e., the main-character-as-subjects and important-action-as-verb rules). Readers will prefer a passage’s overall cohesion than the clarity of individual sentences. (Fortunately, most times they are complement rather than conflicting.)

  • When you use words (such as this, that, such, another, the first, the second, etc.) to refer to something in a previous sentence, try to put them at or close to the beginning of a sentence.

Coherence: A Sense of Whole

Coherence can be thought as seeing what all the sentences in a piece of writing add up to, the way all the pieces in a puzzle add up to the picture on the box.

  1. Before you begin writing, name the things you are writing about. Those are your topics.
  2. Topics should be short, concrete, familiar words, and more often than not, they should name the main characters in your story.
  3. Most of your subjects should be topics.
  4. Most important, be consistent: do not vary your subjects for the sake of variety.

In summary, make the correspondence between these three things clear to your reader:

Subjects <—–> Topics <——> Main Characters.

Dignosis and Revision: Topics

  1. Diagnose
    a. Underline the first seven or eight words of every sentence in a passage, stopping when you hit a verb.
    b. If you can, underline the first five or six words of every clause in those sentences.
  2. Analyze
    a. Do the underlined words constitute a relatively small set of related ideas? Even if you see how they are related, will your readers?
    b. Do the underlined words name the most important characters, real or abstract?
  3. Rewrite
    a. In most (not necessarily all) of your sentences, use subjects to name their topics.
    b. Be sure that those topics are, in context, familiar to your readers.

  • [EXAMPLE 1]

    • [BAD] Consistent ideas toward the beginnings of sentences, especially in their subjects, help readers understand what a passage is generally about. A sense of coherence arises when a sequence of topics comprises a narrow set of related ideas. But the context of each sentence is lost by seemingly random shifts of topics. Unfocused, even disorganized paragraphs result when that happens.
    • [GOOD] Readers understand what a passage is generally about when they see consistent ideas toward the beginnings of sentences, especially in their subjects. They feel a passage is coherent when they read a sequence of topics that focus on a narrow set of related ideas. But when topics seem to shift randomly, readers lost the context of each sentence. When that happens, they feel they are reading paragraphs that are unfocused, even disorganized.

  • [EXAMPLE 2] The below example is full of problems we have mentioned till now: too many nominalizations, stiff verbs, absent characters, cumbersome subjects, etc., and of course, incoherence.

    • [BAD] The importance of language skills in children’s problem-solving ability was stressed by Jones (1985) in his paper on children’s thinking. Improvement in nonverbal problem solving was reported to have occurred as a result of improvements in language skills. The use of previously acquired language habits for problem articulation and activation of knowledge previously learned through language are thought to be the cause of better performance. Therefore, systematic practice in verbal formulation of nonlinguistic problems prior to attempts at their solution might be an avenue for exploration in the enhancement of problem solving in general.
    • [GOOD] In his paper on children’s thinking, Jones (1985) stressed the importance of language skills in children’s problem-solving ability. He reported that when children improved their language skills, they improved their ability to solve nonverbal problems. Jones thinks that they performed better because they used previously acquired language habits to articulate problem and activate knowledge learned through language. We might therefore explore whether children could learn to solve problems better if they systematically practice how to formulate them.

Avoid distractions at the beginning of a sentence

Eliminate those throat-clearing words when beginning a sentence:

  • [BAD]: And, therefore, it’s important to note that, in Eastern states since 1980, acid rain has become a serious problem.
  • [GOOD]: Since 1980, therefore, acid rain has become a serious problem in the Eastern states.

Reference

  • Joseph M. Williams and Joseph Bizup, Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace (11th Edition)
